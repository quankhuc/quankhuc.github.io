Artifact 1 · Workshop One

The Evolution of AI & Machine Learning: A Visual Timeline

Embedded preview. Open the full timeline in its own page

Title
The Evolution of AI & Machine Learning: A Visual Timeline. Eight decades of AI in one interactive picture, from McCulloch & Pitts (1943) to today's frontier-model race.
Introduction
One screen, eight decades. I laid the history out so you can see AI's boom-and-bust rhythm at a glance instead of reading through a wall of dates.
Description
A hand-coded HTML/CSS timeline. I grouped the milestones into eight eras, shaded the two "AI winters," put the boom years in color, and marked 2012 (AlexNet) as the turning point where algorithms, hardware, and data finally lined up. You read the whole arc top to bottom.
Objective
I built it for AIML-500 Workshop 1 to answer something most AI histories skip: why did the field stall out twice? So I made it an argument, not a list. The winters are shaded, the booms are in color, and 2012 is the hinge everything turns on.
Process
I pulled milestones from a documentary history of AI, the IEEE Computer Society's "Evolution of AI," and Our World in Data's "Brief History of AI." I had Claude draft a first outline, then checked every date against those sources, and found one Claude got wrong. That mistake became the whole point of the piece: verify what the machine hands you. Then I grouped everything into eight eras and laid it out so the boom, winter, boom pattern jumps out.
Tools and Technologies Used
Hand-coded HTML and CSS, no site builder or template. Claude as a research assistant, with every date cross-checked against IEEE and Our World in Data.
Value Proposition
It gives you a two-minute mental model of how AI actually moves: the big jumps come when algorithms, hardware, and data line up, not from a clever idea on its own. That's a handy filter for reading today's hype.
Unique Value
Most AI timelines are just a list of dates. This one has a point of view: it leans on the two winters to explain why progress stalled. And catching Claude's wrong date is exactly why the piece ends up being about not trusting machine output blindly.
Relevance
AI hype is loud right now. Knowing the real history helps you tell a genuine breakthrough from a press release, and the habit of checking claims is worth more than the timeline itself.
References
IEEE Computer Society, "The Evolution of AI"; Our World in Data, "The Brief History of Artificial Intelligence"; a documentary history of AI used to cross-check dates.

Artifact 2 · Workshop Two

Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning: Matching the Method to the Problem

Embedded preview. Open the full report (PDF)

Title
Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning: Matching the Method to the Problem. A short decision guide, built on two opposite cases: email spam filtering and diabetic-retinopathy screening.
Introduction
Anyone can define machine learning and deep learning. The real question on a project is narrower: which one does this problem need, and can you defend the call? That's what this report works through.
Description
A short report (PDF) built around two cases that pull in opposite directions: spam filtering and reading retinal scans. For each one I argue why a method fits and why the other is the wrong tool, back it with real systems, and boil it down to a single rule.
Objective
Written for AIML-500 Workshop 2. Rather than just define the two, I wanted the harder answer: which does a given problem actually need, and why? So I picked two cases where the answer is clear-cut, and for each one I also say why the other method would be a mistake.
Process
I started from the Module 2 distinction: classic ML runs on features a human picks by hand, deep learning learns them straight from raw data. Then I chose two cases that argue themselves. Spam goes to ML; reading retinal scans goes to deep learning. I made the case both ways for each, grounded it in real deployments (Gmail blocks over 99.9% of spam; Google Health's model matched board-certified ophthalmologists, per Gulshan et al., JAMA, 2016), and ended on one decision rule.
Tools and Technologies Used
A written report exported to a shareable PDF. The Module 2 lesson as the anchor, plus real sources to check my claims (Gulshan et al., JAMA 2016; Google's own spam-filtering numbers).
Value Proposition
It shows I can right-size an AI solution: spot when a cheaper, explainable model beats a neural network, and back the call with evidence. Next to the timeline, which shows I can research and explain AI, this shows the other half of the job, picking the right tool and defending it to a room that's half technical and half not.
Unique Value
I didn't list differences. I stress-tested each choice from both sides, the case for the right method and the case against the wrong one, using two cases picked as opposites so the rule falls out on its own.
Relevance
Teams reach for deep learning by reflex and pay for it in data, compute, and models nobody can explain. Choosing the right method is one of the least glamorous and most expensive calls in applied AI.
References
Gulshan et al., "Development and Validation of a Deep Learning Algorithm for Detection of Diabetic Retinopathy in Retinal Fundus Photographs," JAMA, 2016; Google's spam-filtering statistics (Safety Center / Workspace); AIML-500 Module 2 lesson.

Artifact 3 · Workshop Three

Leading Adaptive Change in AI/ML Rollouts

Embedded preview. Open the full discussion (PDF)

Title
Leading Adaptive Change in AI/ML Rollouts: a change-leadership case study built on one real project and one hypothetical, arguing that adoption failure is a feedback-loop problem before it's a model problem.
Introduction
Shipping the model isn't the same as the change succeeding. This piece takes the harder half of that seriously: a technically working AI/ML system can still fail if nobody's watching whether people are actually using it.
Description
A workshop discussion written up as a change-leadership case study. I walk through a real project at WWT instrumenting Amplitude analytics for our marketing team, then stress-test the same idea against a harder hypothetical: a university AI-adaptive-learning platform whose usage data shows it's reaching the wrong students.
Objective
Written for AIML-500 Workshop 2's discussion on adaptive learning in change leadership. I wanted to go past "be flexible" as advice and show what adaptive leadership actually looks like once an AI/ML rollout's adoption data stops matching the plan.
Process
I anchored on a real project: instrumenting Amplitude analytics across wwt.com so marketing could see campaign attribution and engagement. Looking back, I named the gap we missed at the time using Heifetz and Linsky's distinction between technical and adaptive challenges: the rollout itself, standing up the tracking and dashboards, was a technical success. The adaptive challenge, getting the team to actually change how it trusted and acted on the data, is the part we treated as finished instead of round one of a feedback loop. Then I stress-tested the same idea against a harder hypothetical: a university AI-adaptive-learning platform where usage data splits by student group, and the students who need it most use it least, another technical rollout outrunning the adaptive work around it. I worked out what an adaptive leader does with that signal, feedback sessions, usage-data review by segment, revised communication, instead of calling it a failed rollout. AI use is disclosed: I ran the draft past Claude for a critical pass (see Feedback in the reflection); ChatGPT, via Devin, helped draft the hypothetical scenario; the real-project analysis is unassisted.
Tools and Technologies Used
The Module 2 discussion prompt on adaptive learning in change leadership; my own experience instrumenting Amplitude analytics at World Wide Technology; Claude, for a critical feedback pass on this write-up; ChatGPT (via Devin), disclosed, for the hypothetical scenario only.
Value Proposition
Most people evaluating AI/ML talent look for who can build the system or pick the right method. Fewer check for who can tell when a working system is still failing because nobody built the feedback loop around it. This shows I bring that read to AI/ML work: I can lead the rollout, not just ship the build.
Unique Value
It's grounded in something I actually did, not just a hypothetical, and it doesn't flatter the real example. I say plainly where we fell short, treating the launch as finished, instead of writing it up as a win, then use a harder hypothetical to pressure-test the same principle.
Relevance
AI/ML rollouts fail on adoption more often than they fail on the model. Reading a stalled rollout as a signal to investigate, instead of a resistance problem to push through, is a leadership skill teams need as much as any modeling skill.
References
Heifetz, R., & Linsky, M., Leadership on the Line (Harvard Business Review Press, 2002), the technical/adaptive distinction this piece is built on; AIML-500 Module 2 discussion prompt, Adaptive Learning in Change Leadership; my own project experience at World Wide Technology; ChatGPT (via Devin), disclosed AI assistance for the hypothetical scenario.

Artifact 4 · ML Team Communication

Making the Call Clear: Reproducible Work Starts with a Precise Handoff

Embedded preview. Open the full reflection (PDF)

Title
Making the Call Clear: Reproducible Work Starts with a Precise Handoff. A reflection on how one vague instruction can split an ML team across different data and make its results impossible to compare.
Introduction
A model can be trained correctly and still produce a useless comparison if the team uses two versions of the data. This piece looks at the communication failure that can cause exactly that problem.
Description
A one-page leadership reflection built around an instructor-authorized hypothetical ML group project. One vague request sends three teammates down different paths: one cleans the newest raw dataset, another trains on an older normalized file, and the third waits because no owner was named.
Objective
I wrote this for AIML-500 to examine how my direct, task-focused communication style could create technical errors during collaborative ML work. My goal was to turn a broad lesson about clarity into a concrete workflow lesson about ownership, data inputs, expected outputs, and deadlines.
Process
I started with one deliberately incomplete message: "Please finish preprocessing and run the baseline model by Thursday." I traced every detail it leaves unresolved, including the owner, dataset version, preprocessing steps, output, and exact deadline. Then I followed the consequences through to the team meeting, where metrics cannot be compared and work has to be repeated. The revision turns those gaps into a practical communication check: name the owner and input, define what finished means, set a precise deadline, and confirm that each person understood the same assignment. At my instructor's direction, I used an AI assistant to help create and organize the hypothetical incident. The PDF labels that assistance and does not present the scenario as a real event.
Tools and Technologies Used
The AIML-500 reflection prompt, a machine-learning workflow involving preprocessing and baseline-model training, 1 Corinthians 14:8–9 and Genesis 11:6–7 as reflection sources, and an AI assistant used under instructor direction with disclosure.
Value Proposition
This artifact shows that I treat communication as part of model quality. Before a team compares metrics, everyone needs the same data, steps, ownership, and definition of done. Making those details explicit prevents wasted runs and makes the result easier to reproduce.
Unique Value
Instead of discussing communication as a general soft skill, the reflection connects ambiguity to a specific technical failure: two valid-looking model runs whose metrics cannot be compared because their inputs differ.
Relevance
ML work is collaborative and dependent on consistent inputs. A precise handoff can be the difference between a reproducible experiment and a meeting spent tracing which file each person used.
References
1 Corinthians 14:8–9; Genesis 11:6–7; AIML-500 communication-reflection prompt; the instructor-authorized hypothetical ML team scenario included in the artifact.
